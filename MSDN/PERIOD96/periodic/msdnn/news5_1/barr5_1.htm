Removing Barriers: Microsoft Publishes Guidelines for More Accessible Software

by Gregory C. Lowney

In the November/December 1995 issue of Developer Network News, we talked about one of the tips you can use to make sure your application doesn’t put up unnecessary barriers for users with disabilities. This month we’re celebrating a major milestone in this effort—publication of the Windows Guidelines for Accessible Software Design, which can be found in the January 1996 release of the Microsoft Development Library.

Microsoft is committed to making computers easier for everyone to use. To achieve that goal requires everyone’s cooperation in addressing the needs of individuals with disabilities.

This goal is partly a reflection of society’s realization that people who have disabilities are a valuable resource. Society as a whole benefits when we take full advantage of every talented computer programmer, artist, engineer, writer, accountant, or teacher. This has been proven time and again, not only by such prominent examples as Stephen Hawking, but by the millions of people who have acquired a disability but continued to play important roles in their work. Computers—and specifically computer hardware and software that is designed to be inclusive—are making that possible in new ways

Accessibility at All Levels

Computer accessibility affects design at all levels: hardware, operating system, and applications.

The Windows 95 operating system sets a new standard by incorporating more built-in functions designed to create a level playing field than any previous operating system. Some of these are easy to spot, such as the Accessibility Options in Control Panel, but others are subtle because they don’t look like they’re for people with disabilities. They look like they’re for everyone, and they are. Most technologies designed to be more inclusive make things easier for everyone.

In general, Windows 95 itself is pretty accessible, but there is a lot of variability among applications. The main reason is that almost all applications are designed without considering the needs of people with disabilities. That doesn’t necessarily mean they’re bad or inaccessible, but it does increase the chances that design and implementation details may put up barriers the designers never intended.

Every application has some of these inadvertent problems. Some recent examples of difficulties that people with disabilities face include:

• Special characters in a list box are always drawn in black. When the item is highlighted with the normal dark blue, it’s impossible to differentiate.

• A dialog box that can’t be traversed with the tab key, because every time you reach a certain control you get "trapped" when another dialog box pops up.

• A feature that can only be configured by clicking the right mouse button. Not only is this a problem for people who can’t use a mouse and for people using one-button pointing devices, but such a feature is almost impossible to discover on your own.

• An application that shows important instructions only in a bitmap. For users who are blind, it presents a blank wall with no door, even though it’s simple to expose the underlying text without altering the visible user interface.

Problems like these are very common. They’re also very easy to remedy, once you have a basic familiarity with the issues and the solutions.

The Accessibility Guidelines

It’s often just as easy to implement a solution that’s accessible as one that imposes barriers. That’s why Microsoft is publishing the guidelines for accessible software design.

The accessibility guidelines represent the first time a software company has attempted to formalize recommendations that affect all types of users with a wide range of disabilities. Much of the original research was done by the Trace Research and Development Center at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, with funding from the National Institute on Disability and Rehabilitation Research (NIDRR).

Microsoft has avidly participated in this project, expanding on the original concepts and adding recommendations specific to the Windows environment. These are based on its experience, plus that of other mainstream software developers, with developers of accessibility aids and with end users.

The guidelines introduce the various types of disabilities and how people affected make use of the computer. It also provides some of the economic reasons why accessible programming makes sense to any business. The technical sections describe the specific problems you may encounter and solutions available. Areas discussed include:

• Basic principles of accessible software design.

• How to provide good keyboard access for users who cannot use a pointing device.

• How to expose the focus location so accessibility aids can present relative information to the user (the topic in the November/December 1995 issue of Developer Network News).

• Using controls and menus so that they’re compatible with accessibility aids that need to simulate input (such as voice recognition), or represent the controls to the user who’s blind or has low vision. These steps also allow you to use automated tools to improve your product testing process.

• How to draw text and graphics to the screen while maintaining compatibility with blind-access utilities.

• How to effectively identify windows to the end user and to accessibility aids.

• How to avoid timing problems for people with slow typing or reaction times, or that might trigger seizures in users with epilepsy.

• How to use color effectively for all users, including those with impaired vision, the 10 percent of all males who are color blind, and those using monochromatic devices.

• Making text and graphics scale well, so that people can adjust the size to suit their vision, dexterity, or screen resolution.

• Using sound so the application won’t become unusable by the tens of millions of people who are deaf or hard of hearing, or those working in noisy environments such as an airplane.

• Avoiding problems when laying out windows and dialog boxes.

• Optional features you can use to go that extra step and make your application especially friendly for users with some types of disabilities.

In addition, the document describes ways you can measure how accessible your application is and identify areas for improvement. There are also lists of resources for getting more information on accessibility.

Take a little bit of time to read these guidelines in the January release of the Development Library (search for "Windows Guidelines for Accessible Software Design"). You should gain the tools you need to dramatically reduce the barriers in your product without a major investment of time.
