Why The Microsoft Network?

Easier access and richer content to open online world to users, businesses

John Williams
Microsoft Online Services Group

March 10, 1995

Rapid, efficient information exchange, regardless of time or place, is increasingly central to the lives of a growing number of people. The integration of electronic mail and local area network-based computing has become integral to how a large part of our society functions—so much so that a personal e-mail delivered from across the globe, or the ability to access a file from someone else's PC in another building, is often taken for granted.

Our reliance on this technology suggests how every computer user would benefit, personally and professionally, by having this degree of access.

The recent explosion of interest in the Internet and commercial online services shows that an enormous number of people agree. Internet usage has tripled, and commercial service memberships more than doubled, in the past two years. New providers and offerings appear almost daily.

Yet despite all the hype, more than 90 percent of the PC-installed base remains "unwired." Even if the installed base of PCs didn't grow, the current 20 percent annual growth rate in new online users would result in less than 60 percent penetration in 10 years. Microsoft researched and built The Microsoft Network (MSN) to accelerate the realization of this great opportunity.

Getting connected

The concept of "wiring" together the tens of millions of PCs around the world is not new. The Internet, local bulletin-board systems, and several broadly available commercial online services have been around for more than a decade.

So, Microsoft's first step was to understand the roadblocks to broader market adoption of online services. After two years of research, what the company discovered is a series of fundamental "disconnects" between what current online technology has to offer and what end users and content providers want.

As with any new medium, online services will gain widespread adoption only if they provide uniquely rich and valuable solutions to customer needs, offered in a more compelling and accessible way than the alternatives.

Online service providers such as MSN have two customer bases to which this equation applies: end- user members and independent content providers.

End users want this technology to be easier to access and use. They need more predictable pricing, and the ability to buy what they really need or want. They also need richer, deeper content that is presented in a more robust, compelling manner.

The content and information providers (IPs) need a business model that caters to a wide variety of different businesses and that promises long-term profitability. IPs need to extend brand identities and current assets. They need better tools and information for managing their businesses. And they need the tools and infrastructure to produce and present their offerings in an easily accessible, interesting way.

The Microsoft Network strategy

Together, the existing online technology and its business model hinder widespread adoption by not meeting the needs of either of these customer communities. MSN attempts to answer these needs, and thus provide an environment that expands the overall online services market.

The MSN strategy revolves around four initiatives driven by the needs of its two customer communities:

1. Viable, long-term business environment

MSN's viability rests on the success of content providers to reach and motivate customers. In a nutshell, if they don't make money, MSN won't make money.

Investment by content providers in online technology is the only way the content that reaches end users will improve. To encourage this, MSN has adopted a new, flexible-platform business model that empowers and rewards independent content and service providers.

Existing online services inhibit the way providers can present their information and services, and limit their profits, often to a small share of a shrinking revenue model based on customer online connection-time charges.

MSN provides a new business environment that puts revenue control entirely in the hands of IPs. This means not only control over variable revenue and pricing models—such as subscriptions, online transactions, advertising subsidies, and ticketed events—but also retaining the majority of the revenues generated by their content and services.

2. Easy, inexpensive access

The need to make technology easier to use is magnified in the arena of online technology, where connectivity to and navigation of vast amounts of data are necessary. To that end, access to MSN is a feature of Windows 95.

By extending the feature set and graphical interface of Windows 95, MSN provides a familiar environment that facilitates exploration and interaction in the online world. In all respects, MSN looks, acts, walks, and talks like Windows 95.

For example, services on MSN can be browsed using the Explorer in Windows 95 or from an icon-based container view. Such actions as downloading files are simple drag-and-drop copy operations. Shortcuts enable personalized and efficient navigation. E-mail and rich-text content documents are managed through the Microsoft Exchange and WordPad applications built into Windows 95. And the same familiar user interface carries through all core communication functionality.

MSN also offers affordable access. By adopting a business model that emphasizes member and content activity rather than connect time, Microsoft plans to deliver local phone-call access worldwide at the lowest possible price.

3. A more compelling online experience

Interaction is the lifeblood of online services. How well the environment is produced, and the quality of content depth and presentation, drive a service's ability to attract and retain members.

MSN supports and promotes personalized discovery and investment for members and content providers alike. A new generation of technology and capabilities lets both groups shape dynamic communities around their own interests, products, and services. Experienced, knowledgeable forum managers will facilitate the expansion and diversity of these communities worldwide.

As with the revenue model, control over brand, product, and business extension lies with the content provider. But MSN will facilitate a higher quality of content and services by providing a new generation of content-publishing tools specifically designed to deliver personalized multimedia online content.

4. World-class communication

Access to communication abilities that transcend the traditional boundaries of time, cost, resources, and geography is one of the primary customer needs MSN was designed to deliver.

To meet customer ease-of-use requirements, MSN extends the Windows 95 communication environment through consistent user interface, OLE support, drag-and-drop management, Explorer navigation, and rich-text formatting.

By fully utilizing the Microsoft Exchange e-mail client in Windows 95 and extending this functionality to bulletin board, file library, and "chat" services, MSN offers easy-to-use communication. It was designed from the ground up to accommodate access to the network from anywhere in the world—and exchange of information between members and content providers anywhere in the world.

Bringing it together

MSN represents an opportunity to combine the assets, skills, and tools of the Windows environment with the online world.

The benefits of building MSN on Windows 95 (client) and Windows NT (server) extend beyond ease-of-use and feature-set gains. The long-term potential for the online medium will, like Windows, unveil itself only through the constant innovation encouraged by an open environment.

From linking desktop applications to online components with Shortcuts, to advanced OLE Automation, to custom messaging API solutions, to existing and new standards that drive your current Windows development efforts, MSN is designed to offer new business opportunities. All of this will be integrated and supported through Microsoft developer-service organizations and events beginning this spring.

Because developers will be a crucial part of making this happen, the Developer Network News plans in future issues to take a closer look at business and informational opportunities for developers on MSN, as well as to provide more details on MSN's tools and technologies.

John Williams is the group manager for market development in the Microsoft Online Services Group.
