Case Study: Business Solution Has Pet-Hospital Startup Purring

Martha Maroney

May 9, 1995

In the world of small-animal veterinary medicine, Dr. Scott Campbell has learned that it's smart to think big. Building on the success of his small-animal veterinary practice in Portland, Oregon, Campbell has developed a rapidly growing collection of pet hospitals in Oregon, California, Washington state, and the Chicago area. These VetSmart Pet Hospital & Health Centers, which offer a full range of veterinary services, are managed entirely through a client-server solution based on the Microsoft BackOffice product family.

When Campbell first pursued the VetSmart idea in 1993, he opted to establish pet hospitals at a number of the nationwide PETsMART superstores. Initially he planned to open 60 hospitals, with each eventually employing six veterinary doctors and 30 support staff.

For such an ambitious venture Campbell knew he needed a comprehensive software solution, one that would effectively automate the systems he had developed at his Portland pet hospital over an eight-year period. As Campbell envisioned it, the VetSmart solution would have to support the quality care, customer convenience, and efficiency that had made his Portland facility successful. It would have to help implement his goal of an essentially paperless office. It also would have to ensure the planned hospitals' compliance with a stringent set of medical, legal, accounting, and quality-assurance requirements.

In addition, the VetSmart solution would have to fit smoothly into a clinical environment, offering familiar interfaces to users ranging from doctors to lab technicians to receptionists, and processing information directly from such sources as stethoscopes and electrocardiogram (ECG) devices. Finally, it would have to act as a marketing tool by educating pet owners about the importance of vaccinations and other veterinary services, a function requiring various media and input/output capabilities, including touch-screen, video, bar coding, sound, and multimedia.

A natural migration to Windows

After launching the development effort, Campbell formed a company called Medical Management International (MMI) to manage both development of the software and the VetSmart pet hospitals, and enrolled the company in the Microsoft Solution Provider program. He was joined at MMI by Mark Howard, a FoxPro database expert and president of Vancouver, Washington-based Software Blueprints, itself among the first members of the Microsoft Solution Provider program.

Howard was familiar with Microsoft products, having programmed in FoxPro and before that FoxBASE for more than a decade. "I had a strong relationship with the Microsoft technical support people, starting with the formation of my business back in 1983," Howard explained. "Whatever the question, I knew we could count on receiving a timely and complete answer." He decided to base the VetSmart solution on FoxPro for Windows and other Microsoft tools.

FoxPro under pressure

With the first VetSmart hospitals scheduled to open in spring 1994, development proceeded rapidly. To create a solution that would eventually total a half-million lines of code, a team of 20 to 25 developers and testers worked 70-hour weeks. Howard himself put in even longer hours. "At times the pressure was so intense it felt like an inferno," he recalled. "When we talked about putting out fires, we felt like we really meant it."

Howard took advantage of such FoxPro tools as Screen Builder, Program Manager, Report Writer, and Query Builder. "Along with FoxPro's English-like language, these tools enabled us to manipulate things very quickly," he said. One advantage was the FoxPro approach to events and methods. "Although you can define fewer events and methods than you can in C++, in FoxPro they're very generic and extendible, so you can do a lot with them."

Although the developers used FoxPro for most of their programming, they also added some code using OLE and the Microsoft Foundation Class Library (MFC) in the Visual C++ development system. Visual C++ provided specialized capabilities, including support for an integrated ECG device. Using a dynamic-link library (DLL) created with Visual C++, the developers were able to speed up the refresh of critical screens. The relative ease of using MFC also proved handy. "Since I'm not ordinarily a C++ programmer, I wouldn't have been able to use the language at all without MFC," Howard said.

Building atop existing applications with OLE

OLE played a major role as well. "OLE helped us build on top of existing applications so we didn't have to develop them from scratch—an absolute necessity considering our schedule," Howard pointed out.

Using OLE to export data from Microsoft Excel into Microsoft Graph, for example, the developers built a sophisticated performance-analysis application. By exporting similar data into Word, they constructed a peer-review application, and by trading data between Word and a specialized vertical product, they incorporated powerful quality-control functionality into the solution. Because the Visual Basic for Applications programming system was not available at the time, the developers implemented these communications through dynamic data exchange (DDE).

Office with Windows

For the foundation of the solution, Howard selected Windows NT and Windows for Workgroups as operating systems. "We wanted the ease of use and administration we knew we would find only in a Windows-based approach," Howard explained. "More important, we wanted a system that was designed from the ground up to meet the integration needs of a major client-server solution, and Windows NT met those requirements on the nose."

Microsoft Office Standard Edition provided desktop-productivity tools and components of key applications, and two new products were added when they became available in early 1994: remote mail, for interhospital communication, and the messaging application programming interface (MAPI), for the electronic-mail interface. With remote mail and the remote access service (RAS) available through Windows for Workgroups, the developers built a powerful set of interhospital communications, not only for e-mail but also for remote administration and daily backups. MAPI supported a programmatic interface to Microsoft Mail—which turned out to be a crucial advantage, according to Howard. "Thanks to MAPI, I was able to build the communications set with minimal coding," he said.

From medical records to inventory

In March 1994, developers released the first version of the VetSmart solution and within six weeks installed it in six new VetSmart pet hospitals. Today, the solution is running in more than 40 VetSmart facilities.

Known as PetWare, the solution incorporates more than 600 screens. On the medical side, it streamlines record entry and management, diagnoses and therapeutic protocols, vaccination history and results, prescriptions, and laboratory record keeping. For each hospital's management, it helps track team assignments and staff performance. Administratively, PetWare supports revenue accounting, invoicing, and inventory management. For marketing, it provides educational handouts and videos, which are played for clients in the exam rooms.

The PetWare server workstations run Windows NT Server, while its client workstations run the Windows for Workgroups operating system. A documentation workstation and a helpdesk workstation run Windows NT Workstation. At each pet hospital a FoxPro database of more than 100 MB provides PetWare's main data engine, which can be accessed by as many as 20 people almost continuously. "There's nothing faster than FoxPro's Rushmore technology for lookup, which is mostly what we do," Howard said. "Users consistently report instantaneous data access."

RAS, in addition to supporting e-mail, provides remote administration of the hospitals' servers and local area networks (operating through NetBEUI protocols) from the MMI office in Portland. "NetBEUI is transparent, reliable, and flexible, and RAS is fantastic for the complete level of control it gives us," Howard said. "In particular the Windows NT command prompt gives us much-needed control over such tasks as monitoring error logs and nightly backup, both critical for meeting the stringent redundancy requirements in the veterinary profession."

The Windows NT scheduler component provides additional network administration services by enabling administrators to schedule software installation, backups, file updates, and other tasks remotely. "With the scheduler we save time, money, and headaches by avoiding all those hours we might otherwise spend on the phone," Howard noted.

Automating remote administration

Immediate plans for PetWare include upgrading all the developer workstations to Windows NT Workstation. Once that happens, administrators will use the Microsoft Systems Management Server, another component of the BackOffice product family, to completely automate remote administration.

In other areas, developers will upgrade the central database engine with Visual FoxPro version 3.0. After that they may replace the corporate data repository, now in a FoxPro database, with SQL Server. Making this possible will be the Visual FoxPro 3.0 direct-command capabilities for SQL Server, which will enable FoxPro to act as a front end to a SQL Server database. "We believe SQL Server can give us even greater data integrity and more secure transaction rollbacks and central storage," Howard said.

According to Howard, a number of major pet-food and pharmaceutical companies have expressed interest in using VetSmart data for research and new-drug trials. "Since these businesses are SQL-oriented, a Microsoft SQL Server platform could make our data much easier for them to access," he said.

An indispensable role

According to Campbell, PetWare plays an indispensable role at VetSmart. "Without PetWare we wouldn't be able to offer the high-quality, consistent, and cost-effective care that's required for a venture of this scope to succeed," he said. "We simply couldn't have created this business without it."

Current plans call for VetSmart hospitals in 120 locations by the end of 1997. As Campbell explained, "The continuing expansion of VetSmart is likely to make it the most visible veterinary practice in the United States and the one by which people judge the whole profession." With PetWare supporting the hospitals' operations, he welcomes the increased visibility. "We're adamant about raising standards, doing the best we can by pets and their people, and helping the profession in general," he said. "Using PetWare, I'm confident we can keep on doing these things."
